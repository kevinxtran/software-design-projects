#### 1. **Understanding Software Quality**

- **Definition:** Software quality refers to a set of parameters used to determine how "good" the software is. However, "good" is relative and depends on **stakeholder expectations**.
- **Stakeholder Expectations:** Different stakeholders (e.g., end-users vs. software designers) may have different views on what makes software "good."
    - **End-users** might prioritize usability and accuracy.
    - **Designers** might focus on **modularity** and **modifiability**.

#### 2. **Software Quality Frameworks**

- Software quality frameworks help to accommodate various stakeholder perspectives. Common frameworks include:
    - **ISO-25010**
    - **SEI-CMM**
    - **McCall’s Quality Factors**
- Focus will be on the **ISO-25010** framework in this context.

#### 3. **ISO-25010 Software Quality Framework**

The framework defines **eight major quality categories** for a software system:

- **Functionality:** How well the software satisfies stated needs or requirements.
- **Compatibility:** The ability to exchange information with other products or software systems.
- **Reliability:** The ability to maintain performance levels over time.
- **Usability:** How easy it is for users to understand, learn, and use the software.
- **Efficiency:** Resource usage considerations (e.g., time, memory, and network usage).
- **Maintainability:** How easily the software can be modified and tested (modifiability).
- **Portability:** Cross-platform compatibility and the ability to work in different environments.
- **Security:** Protection against malicious attacks and the safeguarding of information.

#### 4. **Contextual Application of Quality Factors**

- These quality factors are not universally applicable to every software system; their importance varies depending on the **type of software** and its **use case**.
    - **Example:** Security is critical for medical systems but may not be as important for a mobile game.
- Designers must **apply** these factors in proportion to the specific needs of the software being developed.

#### 5. **Incorporating Software Quality Factors**

- The key challenge is to **measure** and **test** these quality factors during the design phase.
    - Designers need **specific parameters** for each quality factor to guide evaluation.
- **Example of Efficiency:** For a spell checker, a specific quality factor could be that it should check 1000 words in 10 seconds. This defines the **scope**, **task**, and **measurement** clearly.
    - The efficiency of the spell checker can be measured by its performance time.

#### 6. **Measuring and Testing Quality Factors**

- In software design, each quality factor needs a **measurable** and **testable** definition.
- The **spell checker example** illustrates how a quality factor like efficiency is applied in a measurable and specific context.
    - This allows for more **precise testing** and ensures that the software meets the expected standards.

#### 7. **Summary**

- **Software Quality:** Defined as a set of characteristics that relate to either operational behavior or non-operational artifacts, meeting stakeholder expectations.
- **Quality Frameworks:** ISO-25010 is a key framework that categorizes software quality into eight broader factors.
- **Stakeholder-Driven Design:** Each stakeholder’s perspective informs which quality factors are prioritized.
- **Measurable and Testable Factors:** Crucial for successful incorporation of quality factors into software design, allowing designers to test the software against specific standards.

### Takeaways for Study/Review:

- **ISO-25010 Framework:** Focus on the eight quality factors and how they apply to various software systems.
- **Stakeholder Expectations:** Recognize that what constitutes "good" software varies by stakeholder.
- **Measuring Quality Factors:** Understand how to define quality factors in measurable terms (e.g., efficiency measured by time).
- **Contextual Application:** Quality factors must be applied appropriately based on the software type and its usage context.

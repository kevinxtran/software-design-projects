#Week4
- **Software Architecture Design Tactics**: Techniques used to incorporate quality attributes into the software architecture during the design phase. They help achieve desired responses to specific stimuli, ensuring the system meets quality requirements.
    
- **Quality Attribute Scenarios**: These scenarios have two essential components:
    
    1. **Stimulus**: An event or condition that triggers a system's response.
    2. **Response**: The system's behavior or reaction to the stimulus, which should align with the desired quality attribute (e.g., availability, performance).
- **Design Tactics**: Specific design decisions made to achieve the expected response to a given stimulus. These are micro-level design decisions incorporated into the system design to meet quality attributes (e.g., adding redundancy to improve availability).
    
- **Difference Between Design Tactics and Architecture Patterns**:
    
    - **Software Architecture Patterns**: Provide a high-level structure and template for system design. They define the overall framework and structure of the system.
    - **Design Tactics**: Applied at a lower level to refine and adjust an architecture pattern to meet specific quality attributes. Tactics are often embedded within architecture patterns but can also involve adjustments to meet specific system requirements (e.g., adding redundancy in MVC for availability).
- **Example: MVC Architecture and Redundancy**:
    
    - MVC (Model-View-Controller) pattern usually does not include redundancy. However, to improve availability, a tactic such as adding a duplicate model is incorporated. If the original model fails, the system can seamlessly switch to a redundant model, improving the system's availability.
- **Impact of Tactics**: While tactics enhance certain quality attributes (like availability), they often introduce additional overhead, such as:
    
    - **Performance Impact**: The synchronization required for redundancy can affect system performance.
    - **Resource Requirements**: Extra resources (both human and computational) are needed for implementation and maintenance.
- **Categories of Availability Tactics**:
    
    1. **Fault Detection**: Identifying faults before they impact the system (e.g., using system heartbeat checks or monitoring tools).
    2. **Fault Recovery**: Recovering from failures after they occur, using methods such as rollback, active/passive redundancy, or re-introduction modes.
    3. **Fault Prevention**: Preventing faults by removing or isolating faulty subsystems temporarily, which reduces the likelihood of system failure.
- **Design Tactics in Action**:
    
    - Software designers select appropriate tactics based on the quality attribute scenario (e.g., availability, performance, security).
    - **Fault Detection**: Can involve proactive system monitoring and detection of potential faults.
    - **Fault Recovery**: Can involve re-establishing system functions, like having a backup or passive redundancy available.
    - **Fault Prevention**: Involves isolating or temporarily disabling services that might fail.
- **Flexibility in Tactics**: Designers can use pre-existing tactics or develop new ones based on the system's specific needs. They may also combine different tactics to address the quality attributes effectively.
    
- **Importance of Testing**: The framework proposed by Len Bass, Clements, and Kazman emphasizes the importance of testing tactics to ensure that quality attribute responses meet the expectations before deployment.
    

**Summary of Key Points**:

- Design tactics are used to adjust and refine architectural patterns to meet quality attributes (e.g., availability, performance).
- A software system’s architecture may incorporate multiple tactics, and designers must balance costs and benefits when selecting tactics.
- Quality attributes like availability can be improved by tactics such as redundancy, but these may add overhead.
- Testing and evaluation of tactics are crucial to ensuring the system behaves as expected under various conditions.
